\begin{abstract}
During the pandemic caused by the SARS-CoV-2 virus, the UK’s Office for National Statistics ran a large prevalence survey, the Coronavirus Infection Survey (CIS).
Individuals were selected at random with those successfully recruited then tested on a predetermined schedule removing bias by individuals' changing propensity to test and/or infection status.
CIS, mainly designed to provide SARS-CoV-2 prevalence estimates, was a unique resource never previously available during a pandemic.
%removing  most reporting biases.
Here we investigate how this survey can be used to provide deeper insights into the pandemic, including estimates of incidence and transmission.
To do this, we employ complementary modelling approaches, trading off bias and variance, and unravelling different aspects of the transmission dynamics.
We call for further work to optimise prevalence surveys as a leading tool for future pandemic surveillance, with the potential to be more robust and timely than existing methods.
\end{abstract}

\dropcap{T}he pandemic caused by SARS-CoV-2 infections resulted in a horrific toll on humanity, inflicting 31 million deaths~\citep{whoCOVIDExcess,economistCOVIDExcess}, stretching healthcare services to the brink of collapse~\citep{fongNHS}, and disrupting societies worldwide.
The impact motivated governments to invest in infectious disease surveillance on a scale never previously seen. The UK's Coronavirus Infection Survey (CIS)~\citep{cisMethodsONS,CIS}, was a significant example.
Households were selected at random from UK address lists and invited to enrol for regular testing according to pre-defined schedules.
% This design minimizes biases associated with wider population testing, such as the UK's pillar 2 system, which focused on symptomatic individuals and other groups likely to be infected but produced data affected by unmeasured behavioural and socioeconomic factors (\eg willingness and ability of individuals to be tested).
The study design required a level of resource rarely available to infectious disease surveillance systems, but provided data 
unaffected by individuals' ability or propensity to test, and independent of the severity of their disease. Such an unbiased source of information had never previously been available during a pandemic~\citep{shadboltChallenges}.

The CIS was primarily designed to provide weekly estimates of prevalence, namely the proportion of the population that would test positive for SARS-CoV-2 using a Reverse Transcription Polymerase Chain Reaction (RT-PCR) test at a given time. These estimates, stratified by age and region and adjusted for any non-representativeness due to differential response rates among different demographic groups, were published weekly by the Office for National Statistics (ONS)~\citep{pouwelsCommunity,pouwelsMRPvaccination,cisMethodsONS,onsCISdec2020}.
%; these estimates were published weekly by ONS~\citep{pouwelsCommunity,pouwelsMRPvaccination,cisMethodsONS,onsCISdec2020}.
However, prevalence is a delayed indicator of infection and does not provide information on the time at which infections occur, which is key to understand how transmission changes in response to control measures and other interventions~\citep{hallettEstimating}.
More useful is knowledge of incidence of infection, the number of new infections over a specific period, and an understanding of the transmission mechanism.

%knowledge of the incidence of infection, the number of new infections over a specific period, and understannding of the infection transmission mechanism a more useful metric, although much more complex to estimate from the typically available data ({\it{e.g.}} information on severe infection sequelae). Furthermore, reconstruction of the transmission mechanism generating new infections provide an even richer source of evidence to inform response.

A variety of methods exist to estimate infection incidence and transmission, with methods often categorised by how explicitly they represent the underlying biological and epidemiological processes. At one extreme, purely statistical (often referred to as ``phenomenological'') approaches focus on deriving estimates of the rate at which infections occur, making minimal assumptions on the underlying infection and transmission processes~\citep{beckerCOVIDmodels,garnettMathematical}.
This reduces the risk of introducing bias through incorrect assumptions but cannot capture the underlying dynamics of transmission. At the other extreme lie approaches based on mechanistic models, which explicitly include a representation of the process that generates new infections and the disease state of individuals in a population~\citep{andersonInfectious,keelingModeling}.
These models provide deeper insights into transmission dynamics, allowing estimation of the population immunity of different demographic groups and the rate at which susceptible individuals become infected through interaction with infectious individuals.

During the SARS-CoV-2 pandemic, various methods were proposed to estimate incidence and transmission using CIS data, or an additional large survey in the UK, the REal-time Assessment of Community Transmission (REACT)~\citep{rileyResurgence,rileyREACT}.
Some of the approaches~\citep{daviesAssociation,ironsEstimating,knockKey,pooleyEstimation,birrellRTM2} used mechanistic models with age structure, but adopted a coarse approximation of the distribution of the duration of RT-PCR positivity and relied on information from external, possibly biased, surveillance data streams.
One study used a granular geographic scale and a complex observational model but a simplistic transmission model without incorporating age, with the primary aim being the ``debiasing'' of population testing data ~\citep{nicholsonImproving}.
Others took a phenomenological approach~\citep{abbottCISincidence,colmanAscertainment,mccabeCISincidence,ealesAppropriately} without fully propagating the uncertainty in CIS estimates of prevalence.
%, relying on only summary statistics.

In this work, we reconsider estimation of incidence and transmission from CIS data. CIS's comprehensive and consistently collected data offer a unique opportunity to derive critical epidemiological metrics minimally relying on additional data sources. We advance previous work by fitting  both phenomenological and mechanistic models solely to CIS data, provide a more accurate representation of the duration of RT-PCR positivity, using a duration distribution estimated from a representative sample, and fully propagate the survey uncertainty.

In this proof-of-concept study, we show that CIS, designed primarily to measure prevalence, can be the basis of incidence and transmission estimation, and provide guidance on appropriate methodologies for this purpose.
% The phenomenological model provides a flexible framework with minimal assumptions, while the mechanistic model offers detailed insights into transmission dynamics.
We highlight the strengths and limitations of two modelling approaches, then conclude by discussing the remaining barriers to prevalence surveys like CIS serving as a cornerstone for pandemic surveillance and response.

\section*{Results}

We fitted two models to \numprint{1949299} CIS RT-PCR test results, between 31 Aug 2020 and 24 Jan 2021 in England, of which \numprint{20324} (1.0\%) were positive.
%% In this period, CIS had recruited enough individuals and prevalence was high enough that enough data was available to show the potential of the study design.
In order to focus on the base methodology and study design, we have excluded tests beyond this point when the effects of the vaccination roll-out became significant as more than 10\% of the population had received at least one dose~\citep{whoCovidDashboard}.

The first model, a phenomenological model, used test results stratified by region, sex, age group, and ethnicity using Bayesian multi-level regression with post-stratification (MRP) (\textit{Materials and Methods: Phenomenological model}).
It is a three-step process: first, a Bayesian multi-level logistic regression model estimates prevalence by strata, borrowing information between them and accounting for non-response bias; then a deconvolution calculates the incidence proportion in each stratum implied by the prevalence; and finally, the incidence proportions are poststratified .
We include granular stratification and penalize over-fitting through the use of hierarchical priors.

The second model, a semi-stochastic ordinary differential equation (ODE)-based mechanistic model consisting of two sub-models (\textit{Materials and Methods: Mechanistic model}), is illustrated in the schematic diagram in \cref{fig:SEIR}.
The first sub-model is an SEIR-based transmission sub-model tracking the population fractions that are Susceptible, Exposed (infected but not yet infectious), Infectious, and Removed (no longer infectious but playing no further part in transmission, primarily due to immunity).
The assumption of perfect immunity is suitable over the time period we consider~\citep{weiRisk,murchuReinfectionRisk}.
The second is an observation sub-model, linking the transmission sub-model to the observed RT-PCR test results in CIS.
The model is stratified by age and specified by geographical area (region), making the assumption that infection processes in the different regions are independent reasonable when infection is widespread (REF?).
Stratification incorporates some heterogeneity and survey non-response.
Susceptible individuals in age group $a$ at time $t$ become exposed at a rate $\lambda_a(t)$, which is a function of: relative susceptibility of different age groups; time-varying contact rates that combine pre-pandemic surveys with real-time data on mobility and time-use; the proportion of the population currently infectious; and a transmission component $\beta(t)$ that evolves stochastically over time to account for unmodelled phenomena, such as changes in the mix of circulating viral variants and adherence to pandemic mitigation measures (\eg mask-wearing) (\textit{Materials and Methods}).

% The mechanistic model decomposes changing transmission into three components: changes in contact patterns, measured through Google Mobility data; changes in population immunity; and a remaining unexplained component, which includes behavioural changes such as masking or improved case-finding leading to a higher proportion of infectious individuals being isolated and biological changes (\eg variants).

Upon infection, newly infected individuals progress from the susceptible into the first exposed state (state $s$ to $e_1$) and to further latent disease states with rates $\sigma$ and $\gamma$, the reciprocal of the average period of latent infection and the average infectious period respectively.
Copies of infected individuals are introduced into the observation sub-model in the pre-positive state ($p_0$).
Parameter $\omega_0$ is related to the mean time from exposure to having a detectable infection, and $\omega_1$, $\omega_2$, $\omega_3$, and $\chi$ are parameters introduced to accurately reproduce the duration distribution of PCR positivity, based on prior estimates~\citep{blakeDuration}.
Unlike the phenomenological approach, model parameters now have a direct physical interpretation.
Therefore, the use of the mechanistic model can give meaningful insight on quantities impacting transmission (\eg the relative susceptibility of children compared to adults).

We fit both models in a Bayesian framework (\textit{Methods and Materials}), hence results are given as posterior median and 95\% central credible interval (CrI).

\subsection*{Incidence estimates}

Both models produce estimates of SARS-CoV-2 incidence proportion, the proportion of the population that becomes RT-PCR positive on a given day (\cref{fig:incidence-Eng}).
Results show a slow rise and plateau from Sep 2020 to early Nov 2020 with a peak incidence proportion of 0.081\% (0.073--0.091\%) using the phenomenological model or 0.072\% (0.067--0.076\%) using the mechanistic model, a decrease during Nov 2020, then a fast increase in incidence peaking on 24 (21--28) Dec 2020 at 0.16\% (0.14--0.19\%) using the phenomenological model or on 21 (21--21) Dec 2020 at 0.15\% (0.14--0.16\%) using the mechanistic model.

\subsection*{Incidence by age}

By age, the two models agree on the general trends and credible intervals for incidence proportion almost always overlap in ages 26 and up (\cref{fig:incidence-strat}(B)).
The timings of peaks coincide across age groups, and the highest peak is in the 12--16 age group.
There is some disagreement on the incidence proportions for the under 26 age groups, in differing directions at different points.
The likely driver of this is data on contact rates, used only in the mechanistic model.
Contact rates decrease significantly for 12--16 year-olds during the school half-term break at the end of October, as they have many contacts at school.
The decrease in contact rates flows through into the incidence estimates from the mechanistic model, with a drop to 0.076\% (0.070--0.081\%) from 0.18\% (0.16--0.19\%) between 18 Oct and 1 Nov 2020.
On the other hand, the phenomenological model estimates incidence to be flat in these age groups over this period.
However, the cumulative incidence over this period is almost identical between the models, at 1.6\% (1.5--1.8\%) for the phenomenological model and 1.6\% (1.5--1.7\%) for the mechanistic model.

\subsection*{Susceptibility by age}

An advantage of the CIS dataset is that it both captures all age groups two years old and over and has minimal bias.
The mechanistic model can exploit this data to estimate how individual susceptibility differs by age.
Here, individual susceptibility is defined as the probability of a susceptible individual becoming infected upon contact with an infectious individual which includes both biological factors (\eg differences in immune response) and behavioural factors (\eg differences in hygiene).
We estimate this parameter independently for each region, showing that children aged 11 or younger are less susceptible by around 20\% than the rest of the population in all regions (\cref{fig:children}).
Our estimates for the susceptibility of children under 11, relative to adults, range from 0.69 (0.60--0.81) in South West England to 0.90 (0.83--0.96) in South East England.

\section*{Discussion}

We have demonstrated how prevalence studies like the CIS can be used to estimate incidence of infection and the properties of the process of infection transmission.
To do so we applied two different approaches: a phenomenological model and a mechanistic model, both of which were effectively calibrated using CIS data.

We have shown that our incidence estimates are largely robust to model assumptions, by performing analyses with two very different models.
The robustness is reinforced by using CIS, a study with a consistent, well-understood sampling design targeting nearly the entire population.
In CIS, non-response biases are accounted for in the analysis; and high post-recruitment retention rates limit changes in biases over time, so that the trends inferred are robust.
These characteristics are not shared by other data sources, such as community testing data or data on severe infections.
Community testing data provides an inconsistent signal on infection incidence because they are reliant on the number of tests and who is tested which varies over time due to measured (\eg testing capacity) and unmeasured factors (\eg public perception of risk).
Data on severe infections, such as hospitalisations, tends to be consistent but can be affected by unrecorded changes in admission policies and bed availability~\citep{fongNHS}.
Furthermore, severe infections primarily occur in groups with greater frailty (\eg elderly and other vulnerable groups)~\citep{wardSero,bhopalChildren} meaning that estimates of transmission in young adults and children must be based on extrapolations using strong model assumptions or indirect evidence, such as contact surveys or mobility data.
Here, we exploit the CIS's near-representative recruitment of all age groups over two years old to understand how individual susceptibility differs by age, and show that children are less susceptible than adults.


Our study reinforces the value of using different models with different assumptions to estimate the same quantity.
The mechanistic model provides more useful insights, by decomposing the components of transmission, allows estimation  of age-specific susceptibility,and provide more precise estimates. 
However, the phenomenological model is more flexible; can better fit the data, particularly in granular sub-populations; and it includes ethnicity as a covariate, allowing an equitable response to the pandemic and a better adjustment for the non-response bias in the survey.
An example of this is that the mechanistic model does not always capture all changes in incidence, especially when changes in transmission differ between age groups.
Therefore it appears to compromise between the 12--16 and 17--25 age groups in opposite directions: overestimating the prevalence and incidence in the 12--16 age group and underestimating it in the 17--25 age group (\cref{fig:gof}).
The phenomenological model is more flexible and therefore has a better fit to the prevalence data in these age groups.
This can be interpreted as similar to the classic bias--variance trade-off: by making stronger assumptions the mechanistic model reduces the variance of the estimates but has the potential to introduce bias if those assumptions are violated.
Alternatively a more flexible mechanistic model could be developed to allow these changes to be captured.
A more flexible model would require more computationally-efficient inference, such as Hamiltonian Monte Carlo or approximate Bayesian methods~\citep{strumbeljPresent}.
As well as allowing for more flexible models, which would capture age-specific changes in transmission over time, such work would allow faster model fitting, a high-priority when responding to an emergency such as a pandemic.

Our analysis has important implications for future pandemic preparedness and response. We have shown that a prevalence survey with randomly-selected participants is extremely useful for monitoring an ongoing pandemic with clear advantages over other data collection methods.
Yet these surveys, testing individuals following a pre-determined schedule independent of symptoms, were globally rare during the SARS-CoV-2 pandemic, likely due to their cost.

We showed that our methods can reliably estimate incidence with around a 2-week lag,  and often with a 1-week lag.
This is comparable or better than  methods used in real-time during the pandemic~\citep{spimConsensus,richardsModellingUKReport}, which had around a 2-week lag.
Furthermore, there is potential for an extended model to produce more timely estimates.
When our estimates were inaccurate at a 1-week lag, they were often inaccurate in the same direction across all regions; therefore, borrowing information between regions would likely further reduce the lag.
Another possible improvement is to address that inaccurate estimates occurred most often when behavior changes are large (\eg following the announcement of a national lockdown).
These changes may be better modelled by replacing the Gaussian random walk with a distribution with fatter tails (\eg t-distribution).
To implement these changes, a more efficient inference algorithm is likely required, but implementing this would allow more information to be extracted from CIS-like studies.
If this potential can be realised, CIS-like studies will be able to form a major component of surveillance in future pandemics, and be more robust than current methodologies.

\subsection*{CIS study design and data}
The CIS~\citep{CIS} was set up in April 2020 as a large-scale, longitudinal study.
Individuals were originally recruited in England from those who had previously responded to ONS survey(s); it was expanded in September 2020 inviting a random sample of addresses across the whole UK on an ongoing basis.
Following enrolment and consent, individuals were visited on a pre-determined schedule.
At each visit, a questionnaire was completed and a combined throat and nose swab was collected for RT-PCR testing.
We consider only the result of the RT-PCR testing.
Enrolment was continuously ongoing until 31st Jan 2022, with data collected until 13th Mar 2023~\citep{weiRisk}. 

We use all negative and positive tests in England between Mon 31 Aug 2020 and Sun 24 Jan 2021 for this proof-of-concept study.
Void results, where it could not be determined if the individual was positive or negative, are excluded throughout.
We assume void results are uninformative, as in previous work (CITE).
%% During this period, the CIS was of sufficient size, and transmission was sufficiently prevalent, to provide enough data for the analyses.
%% Second, incidence began to rise over this period, after the fall due to the first lockdown.
This period is chosen to end before substantial vaccine roll-out, which affected many important aspects of the analysis; most relevantly, the duration of detectability and the increase in immunity in the population.
We grouped tests by region and age for both analyses.
In the phenomenological model, we also grouped tests by sex and ethnicity, due to there being evidence of differential non-response in these groups~\citep{pouwelsCommunity}.
However, the additional stratification is not possible in the mechanistic model due to computational constraints and a lack of contact data for these groups.
For regions, we used the nine regions of England (International Territorial Level 1); these are: North East, North West, Yorkshire and the Humber (commonly abbreviated to Yorkshire), East Midlands, West Midlands, East, London, South East, and South West~\citep{onsRegions}.
For age groups, we used under 12 (pre-school and primary school), 12--16 (compulsory secondary school), 17--25 (adolescents and young adults who may remain in formal education), 26--50 (younger working age adults), 51--70 (older working age adults), and 71+ (elderly).
We used two levels of ethnic grouping.
Broad groupings (White and non-White), because the White population makes up over 80\% of the UK, and more granular groupings (Asian, Black, Mixed, White, and Other).


\subsection*{Phenomenological model}\label{sec:MMphenom}
The phenomenological model consists of three steps.
First, a Bayesian multi-level logistic regression model is fit to the data to estimate the probability of a positive test for a variety of population strata (described below) on each day considered.
Next, deconvolution (described below) is used to calculate the incidence proportion in each stratum on each day implied by the probability of a positive test for each of 500 samples from the approximate posterior of the regression model.
Finally, the incidence proportions are poststratified to the population and subpopulations of interest to estimate the incidence proportion in each subpopulation on each day.
The full algorithm for this procedure is given in the SI Methods.

\subsubsection*{Regression model}
We modify a previously-used regression model~\citep{pouwelsMRPvaccination} for the same dataset to be suitable for estimating incidence.
The model is fit independently to each region in a Bayesian framework using the Integrated Nested Laplace Approximation (INLA, implemented in R-INLA)~\citep{RINLA,rueINLA}.
Each day, the probability of a positive test for a given age group, ethnicity, and sex is modelled using logistic regression with fixed effects for sex and White or non-White ethnicity; random effects for time and ethnicity and a two-way random effect for age group and time.
To account for the overdispersion due to the household-based design of the survey and unobserved heterogeneity, we use a beta-binomial likelihood.
See SI Methods for further details.

\subsubsection*{Deconvolution}

Deconvolution for estimating incidence from prevalence was popularized in infectious disease epidemiology by the backcalculation method for HIV incidence estimation~\citep{brookmeyerBackcalculation}.
In this context, it means that the incidence proportions for stratum $i$ on day $t$, $Z_{t,i}$ can be derived sequentially for $t = 1, \dots, T$ from the prevalence on days $1, \dots, t$ $P_{1,i}, \dots, P_{t,i}$ using the forward-substitution method~\citep{cormenMatrix}:
\begin{equation}
    Z_{t,i} = P_{t,i} - \sum_{s=1}^{t-1} Z_{s,i} S(t-s+1)
    \label{eq:deconv}
\end{equation}
where $S(t-s+1)$ is the probability of testing PCR-positive on day $t$ given that the individual was infected on day $s$.
We assume a maximum duration $\dmax$, hence $S(d) = 0$ for $d > \dmax$.
Further details are provided in SI Methods.

\subsubsection*{Post-stratification}
Post-stratification aggregates the incidence proportions in different strata to be representative of a (sub)population, here England or one of its regions.
Specifically, define a (sub)population of interest as a set of strata $\set{S}$.
Then the incidence proportion in the (sub)population on day $t$ is
\begin{align}
Z_t(\set{S})
&= \frac{\sum_{j \in \set{S}} Z_{j,t} N_j}{\sum_{j \in \set{S}} N_j}
\label{backcalc:eq:poststratify}
\end{align}
where $N_j$ is the population size of stratum $j$.

\subsection*{Mechanistic model}
The mechanistic model is an age-structured compartmental SEIR model.
Here the state of the population is represented by the proportion of individuals of each age-group in each of four compartments: susceptible (S), exposed (E), infectious (I), and recovered (R) (\cref{fig:SEIR}).
The model evolves according to a set of ODEs.
Solving the ODEs gives the proportion of the population in each compartment at each time.
The SEIR model is a standard model~\citep{andersonInfectious,keelingModeling}, to which we make two major extensions: allowing the transmission rates between age groups to vary over time and adding an observation model to relate the model to CIS data.

\subsubsection*{Transmission rates}
The rate of transmission between infectious and susceptible individuals has three components: the number of contacts between individuals; the probability of transmission given a contact; and the proportion of the population that is susceptible.

The number of contacts between different age groups were based on POLYMOD~\citep{mossongSocial} and adjusted on a weekly basis using baseline time-use~\citep{UKTUS}, and real-time Google Mobility~\citep{googleCOVID19} and school attendance (unpublished) data according to a previously described method~\citep{vanleeuwenAugmenting,birrellRealtime}.

The probability of transmission between an infectious and a susceptible adult is allowed to vary weekly.
This captures any changes in transmission that cannot be explicitly included in the model (see SI Methods for details).
These probabilities are assumed to be the same for all ages groups except the youngest, where the relative probability of a susceptible child acquiring an infection is allowed to be different from the adult value.

The proportion of each stratum that is susceptible, $\vec{s}(t)$ in \cref{fig:SEIR}, is part of the system of ODEs.

\subsubsection*{Observation model}
The observation model (bottom half of \cref{fig:SEIR}) relates the transmission process to the survey data.

First, a set of states approximates the length of time an individual is positive for SARS-CoV-2 following infection.%; we use a more accurate approximation of this time than much previous work.
These states are then used to calculate the probability of a positive test on each day for each stratum.
Traditionally, compartmental models have used a limited class of distributions used for duration distributions such as these, \eg Erlang distributions~\citep{hurtadoGLCT}.
We instead use an Erlang-Coxian distribution, which allows matching the first three moments of the duration distribution (see SI Methods for definitions).
Therefore, the tail of long-lasting durations is captured more accurately.

Then, the probability of a positive test is used to calculate the likelihood of the data, the number of positive tests on each day in each stratum.
We use a beta-binomial likelihood to capture the clustering caused by CIS's household design (\cref{SEIR:eq:likelihood}).

A full description of the observation model is provided in SI Methods.

\subsubsection*{Inference}
We fit the mechanistic model in a Bayesian framework using Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)~\citep{hastingsMCMC,gelmanBDA}.
Informative priors were used for the starting state of the model and the rates of progression through the model (\cref{table:mech-priors}); this is required for identifiability~\citep{dankwaStructural}.
We used weakly informative priors for all other parameters (SI Methods).
Bespoke Markov chain Monte Carlo code, adapted from a previous study~\citep{ghoshApproximate}, was used to estimate the posterior distribution of the parameters of the mechanistic model.
Proposals were drawn from a multivariate normal distribution, adapted throughout the inference process~\citep{andrieuTutorial} and accepted or rejected based on the Metropolis--Hastings algorithm~\citep{hastingsMCMC}.
All results are based on four Markov chains each of length \numprint{10000000} iterations for each region.
The first \numprint{1000000} were discarded as burn-in, with the remaining chains thinned to every \numprint{5000}th iteration.
This gives \numprint{7200} posterior samples per region.
This was further thinned to \numprint{1800} samples per region when calculating posterior predictive quantities (incidence and prevalence) due to their computational cost.
}

\section*{Supplementary methods: phenomenological model}

Obtaining estimates of incidence through phenomenological modelling consists of three steps.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A Bayesian multi-level logistic regression model is fit to the survey data to estimate the probability of a positive test for a variety of population strata on each day considered.
    \item Deconvolution, based on \cref{eq:deconv}, is used to calculate the incidence proportion in each stratum on each day implied by the probability of a positive test for each of 500 samples from the approximate posterior of the regression model.
    \item The incidence proportions are poststratified to the population and subpopulations of interest to estimate the incidence proportion in each subpopulation on each day.
\end{enumerate}
An overview of steps 1 and 2 are shown in \cref{algo:phenom}.
Step 3 is performed using \cref{backcalc:eq:poststratify}.

\begin{algorithm*}[!h]
    \For{each region $r$}{
        Set $O$ to the approximation of the prevalence posterior for region $r$ computed using R-INLA \;
        \For{$i = 1, \dots, 500$}{
            Set $P^{(i)}$ to a posterior sample drawn from $O$. \;
            \For{each stratum $j$}{
                \For{$t = -\dmax, \dots, 0$}{
                    Set $Z^{(i)}_{r,j,t} = P^{(i)}_{j,1} / \sum_{k=1}^{\dmax} S(k)$ \;
                }
                \For{$t = 1, \dots, T$}{
                    Set $Z^{(i)}_{r,j,t} = P^{(i)}_{j,t} - \sum_{k=-\dmax}^{t-1} S(t - k + 1) Z^{(i)}_{j,k}$ \;
                }
            }
            Set any elements of $\vec{Z}^{(i)}$ that are less than 0 to 0 \;
        }
    }
    \caption{%
        Algorithm for computing 500 posterior samples of prevalence in each strata from the phenomonological model.
        $P^{(i)}_{j,t}$ is the probability of a positive test for stratum $j$ on day $t$ in posterior sample $i$.
        $Z^{(i)}_{r,j,t}$ is the $i$th posterior sample of the incidence proportion for stratum $j$ on day $t$ in region $r$.
        $\dmax$ is the maximum duration of detectability.
    }
    \label{algo:phenom}
\end{algorithm*}

\subsection*{Logistic regression model}

We use a modified version of a previous Bayesian multi-level logistic regression model for the CIS~\citep{pouwelsMRPvaccination}.
The regression model serves two purposes: first, it allows borrowing of information between days and stratum; second, it allows for the correction of non-response bias through the subsequent post-stratification.

For a day $t$, age group $a$, ethnicity $e$, and sex $s$ the regression model is:
\begin{align}
    \logit(p_{taes}) &= \beta + \beta_s + \beta_{w(e)} + u_a + v_e + w_t + l_{a,t} \\
    y_{taes} &\dist \BB(n_{taes}, p_{taes}, \rho).
\end{align}
The terms are as follows.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $n_{taes}$ and $y_{taes}$ are the number of individuals tested and the number of positive test results respectively on day $t$ in individuals of age group $a$, ethnicity $e$, and sex $s$. We use a beta-binomial distribution for the likelihood.
    A beta-binomial distribution is an overdispersed version of the binomial distribution~\citep{hughesUsing}; it has mean $n_{taes} p_{taes}$ and variance $n_{taes} p_{taes} (1 + n_{taes} \rho) (1 + \rho)^{-1}$. 
    Overdispersion allows for household clustering and other unmodelled variation in the data, which is common in epidemiological data~\citep{griffithsBBD}.
    \item $\beta$ is a global intercept term with the vague prior $\beta \dist \N(0, 1/0.001^2)$.
    \item $\beta_s$ is a fixed effect for sex, with $\beta_s = 0$ for males and estimated for females. The prior for $\beta_s$ is vague, $\beta_s \dist \N(0, 1/0.001^2)$.
    \item $\beta_{w(e)}$ is a fixed effect for White or non-White ethnicity. $\beta_{w(e)} = 0$ if $e$ corresponds to White ethnicity and $\beta_{w(e)} = \beta_{n}$ otherwise (\ie non-White ethnic groups).
      The prior for $\beta_n$ is vague, $\beta_n \dist \N(0, 1/0.001^2)$.
      % $\beta_n$ and $\beta_f$ have the default INLA prior for fixed effects\todo{check what this is}.
    \item $u_a$ and $v_e$ are independent normal random effects for age and ethnic group respectively.
    That is, $u_a \dist \N(0, \sigma_1^2)$ with the prior for $\sigma_1$ chosen to be the penalized complexity prior~\citep{simpsonPenalising}, \ie an exponential distribution.
    This provides regularization because its mode is at 0.
    We chose this distribution's parameter such that $\prob(\sigma_1 > 1) = 0.1$.
    Similarly $v_e \dist \N(0, \sigma_2^2)$ with $\sigma_2$ having the same prior as $\sigma_1$.
    \item $w_t$ is a second-order random walk, that is $w_t = 2w_{t-1} - w_{t-2} + \epsilon_t$ where $\epsilon_t \dist \N(0, e^{-\sigma_w})$.
      $\sigma_w$ has a vague prior, $\sigma_w \dist \GamDist(1, 5 \times 10^{-5})$.
    \item $l_{a,t}$ is an age-group specific second-order random walk.
      That is $l_{a,t} = 2l_{a,t-1} - l_{a,t-2} + \epsilon_{a,t}$ where $\epsilon_{a,t} \dist \N(0, \sigma_l^2)$.
      $\sigma_l$ has a vague prior, $1/\sigma_l \dist \GamDist(1, 5 \times 10^{-5})$.
\end{itemize}
The second-order random walk priors favours linear growth in $\logit(p_{taes})$ over time.
For the small values of $p_{taes}$ likely to be encountered, this approximates exponential growth.

\subsection*{Deconvolution}

The relationship between incidence and prevalence is partially stochastic because the time until an infected individual stops being prevalent is random.
However, here a deterministic relationship is assumed, a good approximation of the stochastic one as long as the proportion of the population sampled by prevalence survey is small because the stochastic noise in the sampling process is much greater than that in the relationship between incidence and prevalence; therefore, the latter is negligible~\citep{blakeThesis}.
Re-writing \cref{eq:deconv} in matrix vector notation, $\vec{Z} = \matr{S} \vec{P}$ where $\vec{Z}$ is the vector of incidence proportions at times $1, 2, \dots, T$ for a given stratum; $\vec{P}$ is the vector of prevalence proportions at times $1, 2, \dots, T$ for the same stratum; and $\matr{S}$ is a lower triangular matrix, defined in terms of the survival function $S(d) = \prob(D \geq d)$ where $D$ is the duration of RT-PCR positivity and $S(1) = 1$, as follows:
\begin{align}
    (\matr{S})_{i,j} &= \begin{cases}
        S(i - j + 1) & \text{if } i - \dmax + 1 \leq j \leq i \\
        0 & \text{otherwise.}
    \end{cases} \label{inc-prev:eq:Smatrix}
\end{align}
For this to have a unique solution, $\vec{Z}$ and $\vec{P}$ need the same dimension.
However, we use a period of prevalence which starts after the first infections; these prior infections will still contribute to the prevalence.
We assume that the incidence for $t \leq 1$ is constant.
Therefore, $P_1 = Z_0 \sum_{i=1}^{\dmax} S(i)$ with $Z_t = Z_0$ for $t \leq 1$.
The solution for $\vec{Z}$ can then be found by forward substitution~\citep{cormenMatrix}, as implemented in the innermost loop in \cref{algo:phenom}.

\subsection*{Poststratification}

Poststratification combines posterior estimates of the prevalance in each stratum at each time point to produce an overall estimate of the prevalence in a (sub-)population.
It uses a weighted average to ensure the overall estimate is representative, detailed in the main text.

\section*{Supplementary methods: mechanistic model}

We fit a SEIR model using MCMC to the CIS dataset, as described in the main text.
We model each region fully independently, \ie with no shared parameters or transmission between regions.
As well as the motivation given for the phenomenological model, the assumption of homogeneous mixing within age groups, which we make below, is more plausible for regions than the whole of England.
By modelling each region independently, we assume that the epidemics in each region do not interact.
Clearly, this is not realistic as travel between regions of England is common and unrestricted.
However, except in an epidemic's early stages, the interaction between large regions has a negligible impact on the epidemic's progression~\citep{birrellRealtimea,gogSpatial,eggoSpatial}.
The model consists of two parts: the transmission model, which describes how the disease spreads through the population; and the observation model, which links the transmission model to the prevalence and hence the data (\cref{fig:SEIR}).
The transmission model for each region is a SEIR model, which means that the population is divided into four types of compartments: susceptible ($s$), exposed ($e$), infectious ($i$), and recovered ($r$), each of which is further stratiﬁed by age~\citep{andersonInfectious,keelingModeling}.
The observation model is a set of states that approximate the length of time an individual is positive for SARS-CoV-2 following infection with an initial pre-positive state $p_0$ followed by a series of positive states $p_1, p_2, \dots, p_l$.



\subsection*{Transmission model}
To more accurately represent the natural history of the disease~\citep{wearingAppropriate}, we use two E compartments and two I compartments.

The transmission model is a system of ordinary differential equations (ODEs), as follows.
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \vec{\lambda}(t) &= \matr{\beta} (\vec{i}_1(t) + \vec{i}_2(t)) \\
    \frac{d\vec{s}(t)}{dt} &= -\vec{\lambda}(t) \circ \vec{s}(t) \\
    \frac{d\vec{e}_1(t)}{dt} &= \vec{\lambda}(t) \circ \vec{s}(t) - 2\sigma \vec{e}_1(t) \\
    \frac{d\vec{e}_2(t)}{dt} &= 2\sigma \vec{e}_1(t) - 2\sigma \vec{e}_2(t) \\
    \frac{d\vec{i}_1(t)}{dt} &= 2\sigma \vec{e}_2(t) - 2\gamma \vec{i}_1(t) \\
    \frac{d\vec{i}_2(t)}{dt} &= 2\gamma \vec{i}_1(t) - 2\gamma \vec{i}_2(t) \\
    \frac{d\vec{r}(t)}{dt} &= 2\gamma \vec{i}_2(t) \\
\end{aligned}
\label{SEIR:eq:fullODEs}
\end{equation}
where $\circ$ indicates element-wise multiplication; $\matr{\beta}$ is the Who Acquires Infection From Whom (WAIFW) matrix, explained below; $\vec{s}(t)$ is a vector such that $s_{i}(t)$ is the proportion of stratum $i$ that is susceptible with $\vec{e}_1(t)$, $\vec{e}_2(t)$, $\vec{i}_1(t)$, $\vec{i}_2(t)$, and $\vec{r}(t)$ similarly defined for the two exposed, two infectious, and removed compartments respectively; $1/\sigma$ is the mean latent period; and $1/\gamma$ is the mean infectious period.


\subsection*{WAIFW matrix} \label{SEIR:sec:WAIFW-application}

We use an age-structured SEIR model, which requires a WAIFW matrix, $\matr{\beta}$, to determine the rate of transmission between different age groups, parameterized in a parsimonious way~\citep{andersonInfectious,keelingModeling}.
We decompose the elements of the matrix as $\beta_{aa'} = \kappa_{aa'} q_{aa'} N_{a'} / N_{a}$ where: $\kappa_{aa'}$ is the mean rate at which an individual in stratum $a'$ makes contact with individuals in stratum $a$; $q_{aa'}$ is the probability of transmission upon contact between an infectious individual in stratum $a'$ and a susceptible individual in stratum $a$; and $N_{a}$ is the total number of individuals in stratum $a$.
For many epidemics, where the population behaviour is fairly constant over time, a constant $\matr{\beta}$ is a good approximation.
However, in SARS-CoV-2, population behaviour changed dramatically, both spontaneously (\eg due to fear of infection) and in response to government-imposed restrictions.
Therefore we use a pre-existing model for a time-dependent WAIFW~\citep{birrellRealtime}:
\begin{equation}
    \beta_{aa'}(t) = \kappa_{aa'}(t) q_{aa'}(t) N_{a'} / N_a.
\end{equation}
$N_a$ and $N_{a'}$ are constant because the change in the total population is negligible over the length of time considered here.

We base the contact rates, $\kappa_{aa'}(t)$, from several data sources, using pre-existing methodology~\citep{vanleeuwenAugmenting,birrellRealtime}.
In brief, we start with pre-pandemic baseline estimates of $\kappa_{aa'}$ from the POLYMOD study~\citep{mossongSocial}, and time use from the UKTUS (UK Time Use Survey)~\citep{UKTUS} then modify them each week based on changes in where people spend their time, as measured by Google mobility data~\citep{googleCOVID19} and school attendance data~(unpublished).
$\kappa_{aa'}(t)$ is assumed to be piecewise constant, changing only at the start of each Monday.

The probability of transmission, $q_{aa'}(t)$, needs further decomposition to be identifiable~\citep{andersonInfectious}.
We assume that:
\begin{equation}
    q_{aa'}(t) = \beta(t) q^\text{susc}_{a} q^\text{inf}_{a'}
\end{equation}
where $\beta(t)$ is a time-varying reference probability of transmission; $q^\text{susc}_a$ is the relative \emph{susceptibility} of stratum $a$, \ie how vulnerable to infection individuals in stratum $a$ are; and $q^\text{inf}_{a'}$ is the relative \emph{transmissibility} of stratum $a'$, \ie how likely individuals in stratum $a$ are to pass on an infection.
These measures include both biological differences, \eg due to differences in immune systems, or behavioural, \eg if infants are less hygienic than adults.

Studies of pre-Alpha SARS-CoV-2 suggest that all individuals have similar infectiousness but that pre-adolescent children are less susceptible than adults~\citep{chenRole,vinerTransmission}.
Therefore, we set $q^\text{inf}_{a'} = 1$ for all $a'$, $q^\text{susc}_{a} = q_c$ for 0--11 year olds, and $q^\text{susc}_{a} = 1$ for the remaining strata.
We estimate $q_c$, the relative probability that a child is infected (compared to an adult), given contact with an infectious individual.

The time-varying component, $\beta(t)$, allows for behavioural changes that impact transmission but are not captured by the contact matrices (\eg mask wearing).
We use a random walk prior on $\log\beta(t)$ on the weekly timescale, smoothing its value over time.
This prior encodes that behaviour changes smoothly over time.
\begin{align}
    \beta(t) &= \beta_{\tilde{w}(t)} \\
    \log\beta_{w+1} &= \log\beta_w + \sigma_\epsilon \epsilon_w \\
    \epsilon_w &\sim \N(0, 1)
\end{align}
where $\tilde{w}(t)$ is the week number of time $t$, with week 1 being the first week considered.
Two hyperparameters will be inferred: $\beta_1$, the initial value of the random walk; and $\sigma_\epsilon$, the standard deviation of the random walk.
$\beta(t)$ is the only time-varying fitted parameter.
Therefore, in practice, it will also absorb other effects such as any error in $\matr{\kappa}(t)$.

\subsection*{Observation model} \label{SEIR:sec:observation}

This section explains the novel observation model approach we propose for fitting to the CIS prevalence data.
This will link the transmission component of the model to the CIS prevalence data.
We approximate the convolution which relates incidence and prevalence by adding compartments to the ODEs.
This approach allows an infected, pre-positive compartment, which we denote $p_0$, to be included.

$p_0$ increases at the same rate as individuals flow into $e_1$.
These individuals then transition into a set of compartments, $p_1, \dots, p_l$, each with their own transition rate.
Individuals in compartments $p_1, \dots, p_l$ are detectable; exiting compartment $p_l$ corresponds to the end of the detectable period.

To incorporate flexibility, the length of time in the states $p_1, \dots p_l$ follows an Erlang Coxian (EC) distribution~\citep{osogamiClosed}.
EC distributions are a phase-type distribution that can match the first three moments of any duration distribution using at most one more compartment than the optimal number.
Furthermore, the parameters of an EC distribution can be calculated using closed form expressions from the distribution's desired moments.
The EC distributions  choose $l$ and the parameters such that combined length of stay (\ie time from entry in $p_1$ until exit from $p_l$) to approximate previous estimates of the duration of RT-PCR positivity~\citep{blakeDuration} up to the first three moments (\cref{SEIR:table:ec-params}).
While the use of exponential or Erlang (gamma distributions with integer first parameter) is common in the observation model of compartmental models~\citep[e.g.][]{overtonEpiBeds}, the use of the more flexible EC-distribution, as here, has rarely, if ever, been used in practice~\citep{hurtadoGLCT}.
This broader class of distributions allows the model to capture the tail of the distribution, which is important when incidence suddenly decreases.
The use of the EC distribution add the following equations to the system of ODEs:
\begin{align}
    \frac{d\vec{p}_0(t)}{dt} &= \vec{\lambda} \circ \vec{s}(t) - \omega_0 \vec{p}_0(t) \\
    \frac{d\vec{p}_1(t)}{dt} &= \omega_0 \vec{p}_0(t) - \omega_1 \vec{p}_1(t) \\
    \frac{d\vec{p}_2(t)}{dt} &= \omega_1 \vec{p}_1(t) - \omega_{2} \vec{p}_2(t) \\
    \frac{d\vec{p}_3(t)}{dt} &= \chi \omega_{2} \vec{p}_2(t) - \omega_{3} \vec{p}_3(t)
\end{align}
where $l=3$ and $\omega_0$, $\omega_1$, $\omega_2$, and $\chi$ are parameters of the EC distribution (\cref{SEIR:table:ec-params}).
\begin{table}
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{c c c c c}
        $l$ & $\omega_1$ & $\omega_{2}$ & $\omega_{3}$ & $\chi$ \\
        3 & 0.0820 & 0.126 & 0.0223 & 0.00973  \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Parameters for the observation model.}
    \label{SEIR:table:ec-params}
\end{table}
% end table of ec params

We link the modelled proportion of individuals that are RT-PCR positive to the data using a beta-binomial likelihood to allow for the household clustering.
If on each day $t$, each stratum $a$ is observed to have $y_{at}$ positives out of $n_{at}$ tests, then the likelihood is:
\begin{align}
    \prod_{a,t} \BB (y_{at} \mid n_{at}, \bar{p}_{at}, \rho)
    \label{SEIR:eq:likelihood}
\end{align}
where: $\bar{p}_{at}$ is the mean proportion of individuals in strata $a$ that are RT-PCR positive on day $t$; and $\rho$ is a nuisance parameter controlling the overdispersion of the beta-binomial distribution.
Denoting the proportion of stratum $a$ in state $p_k$ as $p_{ka}(\tau)$, then $\bar{p}_{at} = \int_{t}^{t+1} \sum_{k=1}^l p_{ka}(\tau) d\tau$.

\subsection*{Implementation and parameterisation}

We solve the system of ODEs using Euler's method, discretised at timesteps of $\delta t = 1/2$ days.
That is, if $\vec{x}_t$ is the system state at time $t$ for $t \in \{ 0, 0.5, 1, \dots \}$ then $x_{t+\frac{1}{2}} = x_t + (d\vec{x}(t)/dt) \delta t$.
The mean prevalence on day $t$, used for the likelihood (\cref{SEIR:eq:likelihood}), can be calculated as $\bar{p}_{at} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^l ( p_{kat} + p_{ka,t+1/2} )$.

As in previous work~\citep{birrellBayesian}, we parameterize $\beta_1$ in terms of the initial growth of the epidemic and a parsimonious parameterization of the initial conditions, using the initial proportion of the population that are infectious and the initial proportion that are susceptible only.

Both reparameterizations rely on an assumption that the epidemic is in a steady-state exponential growth phase at time 0. During such an exponential growth phase, all components of the $\vec{e}$, $\vec{i}$ and $\vec{p}$ compartments grow at the same growth rate, $\psi$.
That is:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d\vec{e}_1(t)}{dt} = \psi \vec{e}_1(t) \\
\frac{d\vec{e}_2(t)}{dt} = \psi \vec{e}_2(t) \\
\frac{d\vec{i}_1(t)}{dt} = \psi \vec{i}_1(t) \\
\frac{d\vec{i}_2(t)}{dt} = \psi \vec{i}_2(t) \\
\frac{d\vec{p}_0(t)}{dt} = \psi \vec{p}_0(t) \\
\frac{d\vec{p}_1(t)}{dt} = \psi \vec{p}_1(t) \\
\frac{d\vec{p}_2(t)}{dt} = \psi \vec{p}_2(t) \\
\frac{d\vec{p}_3(t)}{dt} = \psi \vec{p}_3(t).
\end{aligned}
\label{SEIR:eq:exponential-growth}
\end{equation}
This follows directly from \cref{SEIR:eq:fullODEs} if $\vec{s}$ is constant. 

The growth rate $\psi$ is easily identifiable from the data, and therefore a parameterization in terms of $\psi$ should be more computationally efficient.
If this is the initial exponential phase, when $\vec{s} \approx 1$, then the basic reproduction number $\R$ for an SEIR model can be written in terms of either the growth rate $\psi$ or in terms of the model parameter $\beta_1$.

The expression in terms of $\psi$ is~\citep{birrellBayesian,wearingAppropriate}:
\begin{align}
    R_{\psi} = \frac{\psi \left( \frac{\psi}{2\sigma} + 1 \right)^2}{\gamma \left(1 - \frac{1}{\left(\frac{\psi}{2 \gamma} + 1 \right)^2} \right)} \label{SEIR:eq:rtoR}.
\end{align}

In terms of model parameters, the expression for $\R$ is  \begin{align}
    R_{\beta} = \beta_1 D(\matr{\kappa}(t) \circ \matr{q} \circ \matr{N})
    \label{SEIR:eq:betatoR}
\end{align}
where $D(\matr{A})$ denotes the dominant eigenvalue of the matrix $\matr{A}$~\citep{diekmannDefinition,diekmannMathematical}.

Hence we sample $\psi$ using MCMC (see below) then set $\beta_1 = R_\psi / D(\matr{\kappa}(t) \circ \matr{q} \circ \matr{N})$ to give $R_\psi = R_\beta$.

To form a parsimonious parameterization of the initial state, first focus on the initial proportion of the population that is in the infectious compartments.
In the exponential growth phase, the relative proportion of each stratum that is infectious is proportional to the dominant eigenvector of $\matr{\beta}$~\citep{diekmannDefinition,diekmannMathematical}, which we denote $\vec{d}$.
Let $\vec{i}_0 = \vec{i}_1(0) + \vec{i}_2(0)$ be the total proportion of infectious individuals at time zero.
This vector is parameterized in terms of its largest element $i^+$, such that:
\begin{align}
\vec{i}_0 = \frac{i^+}{\max \vec{d}} \vec{d}.
\label{SEIR:eq:i0}
\end{align}

Equating the expressions for $d\vec{i}_2/dt$ in \cref{SEIR:eq:fullODEs} and \cref{SEIR:eq:exponential-growth} and setting $t=0$ to allow substituting in \cref{SEIR:eq:i0} gives:
\begin{align}
    \vec{i}_1(0) &= \vec{i}_0 \left(1 + \frac{2\gamma}{\psi + 2\gamma} \right)^{-1}.
\intertext{By definition of $\vec{i}_0$:}
    \vec{i}_2(0) &= \vec{i}_0 - \vec{i}_1(0).
\end{align}

Equating the expressions in \cref{SEIR:eq:fullODEs} and \cref{SEIR:eq:exponential-growth}, gives the following solution for the initial state of most of the compartments.
\begin{align}
    \vec{e}_2(0) &= \vec{i}_1(0) \frac{\psi + 2\gamma}{2\sigma} \\
    \vec{e}_1(0) &= \vec{e}_2(0) \frac{\psi + 2\sigma}{2\sigma} \\
    \vec{p}_0(0) &= \vec{e}_1(0) \frac{\psi + 2\sigma}{\psi + \omega_0} \\
    \vec{p}_1(0) &= \vec{p}_0(0) \frac{\omega_0}{\psi + \omega_1} \\
    \vec{p}_2(0) &= \vec{p}_1(0) \frac{\omega_1}{\psi + \omega_{2}} \\
    \vec{p}_3(0) &= \vec{p}_2(0) \frac{\pi \omega_{2}}{\psi + \omega_{3}}.
\end{align}

Define a parameter $\vec{\pi}$ as the proportion of each stratum without initial immunity, then $\vec{r}(0) = 1 - \vec{\pi}$.
Finally, to ensure the population proportions sum to one, $\vec{s}(0) = \vec{\pi} - \vec{i}_0 - \vec{e}_1 - \vec{e}_2$.

\subsection*{Inference}
We use an adaptive random-walk Metropolis--Hastings algorithm to compute the posterior distribution (\cref{SEIR:MCMC-algorithm})~\citep{ghoshApproximate,andrieuTutorial}.
All parameters with bounded supports are sampled using transformations with support over the whole the real line.
Parameters which are constrained to the positive reals ($\sigma_\epsilon$, $\rho$, and $q_c$) were sampled on the log scale.
Parameters which are constrained to the unit interval ($i^+$ and $\vec{\pi}$) were sampled on the logit scale.
\begin{algorithm}
 Set $\vec{X}_0$ to an initial value of the parameter vector \;
 $\vec{\mu_0} = \vec{X}_0$ \;
 $\matr{\Sigma_0} = \text{diag}(\vec{\mu_0})$ \;
 $\lambda_0 = 1$ \;
 \For{$i = 1, \dots, M$}{
  sample $\vec{Y_}i \sim \N(\vec{\mu_{i-1}}, \lambda_{i-1}\matr{\Sigma_{i-1}})$\;
  set $\vec{X_i}$ to $\vec{Y_i}$ or $\vec{X_{i-1}}$ using a MH acceptance step\;
  update the proportion of proposals accepted so far, $\alpha$ \;
  \eIf(\tcc{No adaptation for 200 iterations}){$i \leq 200$}{
    $\lambda_i = \lambda_{i-1}$ \;
    $\vec{\mu_i} = \vec{\mu_{i-1}}$ \;
    $\matr{\Sigma_i} = \matr{\Sigma_{i-1}}$ \;
   }{
    $\gamma_i = (i - 200)^{-0.6}$ \;
    $\log \lambda_i = \log \lambda_{i-1} + \gamma_i(\alpha - 0.234)$ \;
    $\vec{\mu_i} = (1 - \gamma_i) \vec{\mu_{i-1}} + \gamma_i \vec{X_i}$ \;
    $\matr{\Sigma_i} = (1 - \gamma_i) \matr{\Sigma_{i-1}} + \gamma_i (\vec{X_i} - \vec{\mu_i})(\vec{X_i} - \vec{\mu_i})^T$ \;
  }
 }
 \caption{Algorithm for adaptive random-walk Metropolis--Hastings. $\vec{\mu_i}$ and $\matr{\Sigma_i}$ are an estimate of the mean and covariance of the posterior distribution using information up to iteration $i$. $\text{diag}(\vec{\mu_0})$ is the diagonal matrix with diagonal entries equal to $\vec{\mu_0}$. $\lambda_i$ is the scale parameter of the proposal distribution at iteration $i$, tuned to try and ensure an optimal proportion of proposals are accepted (23.4\%). $\gamma_i$ is the learning rate, which determines how much adaptation occurs. $\gamma_i \to 0$ as $i \to \infty$ meaning the rate of adaptation is \emph{vanishing}. A vanishing rate of adaptation guarantees the algorithm converges to the target distribution~\citep{andrieuTutorial}.}
 \label{SEIR:MCMC-algorithm}
\end{algorithm}


All results are based on four Markov chains each of length \numprint{1000000} iterations.
The first \numprint{500000} were discarded as burn-in, with the remaining chains thinned to every 100th iteration.
This gives \numprint{5000} posterior samples per region.
This was further thinned to \numprint{2000} samples when calculating posterior predictive quantities (incidence and prevalence) due to their computational cost.

We implement the simulations study and inference code in Python~3.8.2~\citep{python} using pandas~2.0.3~\citep{pandas}, numpy~1.24.4~\citep{numpy}, and scipy~1.10.1~\citep{scipy}.
The strata are larger than those in the phenomenological model, allowing the data to be extracted from the SRS.
Therefore, inference was performed using the University of Cambridge high-performance cluster.

\subsection*{Priors} \label{sec:mech-priors}

Priors for the parameters for the mechanistic model given in \cref{table:mech-priors}.
The parameters governing the duration of detectability were given previously (\cref{SEIR:table:ec-params}).
The initial conditions and transition rate parameters are unidentifiable in an SEIR model~\citep{dankwaStructural}, therefore strongly informative priors or fixed values are used for them.
$\sigma_\epsilon$ and $\rho$ are given priors that favour parsimony.
The prior on $\sigma_\epsilon$ is a penalized complexity prior, favouring a null model of constant values for $\beta(t)$~\citep{simpsonPenalising}.
The prior on $\rho$ favours a null model of a binomial likelihood.

To form the prior for $\vec{\pi}$, we used a pre-existing analysis framework~\citep{amirthalingamSeroprevalence}, applied to Aug 2020 seroprevalence data from blood donors~\citep{amirthalingamSeroprevalence} and a representative sample of children~\citep{ratcliffeCommunity}.
This analysis uses a Bayesian framework to correct the seroprevalence data for the sensitivity and specificity of the serological tests, using an informative prior.
It forms the prior for the tests by evaluating the tests using samples which are known to be negative (taken before the pandemic) or positive (taken from an individual following a positive RT-PCR test).
A regression model predicts the proportion of the population with prior immunity in each age group and region.
We approximate the posterior of this proportion with a beta distribution to form the prior for the mechanistic analysis (\cref{SEIR:table:immunity-prior}).

The remaining parameters have weakly informative priors.
